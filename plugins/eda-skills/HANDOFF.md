Simulated on that structure: individual correlations **+0.36…+0.45** (the real heat map shows 0.72–0.78), nothing near a threshold, while OLS on all seven gives `R² = 1.000000000000`, relative residual **1.07e-15**, versus **0.0992** for an honest model at a true R² of 0.99. **The guard was the real work**: with `n ≤ p+1` least squares fits *any* target exactly, so an unguarded probe cries identity on noise — measured minimum relative residual on pure noise by `n/(p+1)`: **0.0000** at 1.0, 0.0008–0.0720 at 1.2–1.3, 0.246–0.428 at 2.0 — hence a hard refusal below `n ≥ 2(p+1)`. A hit is **a question for the data owner**, not a licence to auto-drop columns.

**(b) Kurtosis is a tail measure, not "peakedness".** `|z|<1` holds **68.26%** of the rows and supplies **3.75%** of `E[z⁴]`. But the honest version matters: peak and kurtosis *are* rank-correlated (Spearman **+0.94** on exact values), which is why the misconception survives — it **inverts** on specific pairs, Laplace peak **0.7071** / kurtosis **+3.000** against t(5) peak **0.4901** / kurtosis **+6.000**, computed analytically after a first KDE pass produced a windowing artifact on a U-shaped beta that was dropped rather than reported. Also: sample kurtosis is worst where it matters (t(5), n=400 000 → **5.337** vs true 6.000).

**(c) Iterated 1.5·IQR removal has no fixed point.** On **clean** lognormal data the loop runs 12 passes, deletes **14.40%** of rows and **68% of the spread** (sd 2.1433 → 0.6866) — all of it the variable's own right tail. Even a normal loses 0.73% on the first pass. "No dots on the box plot" is a statement about the box plot.

**Reported, not adopted (already core):** the article's whole pipeline — median imputation, `drop_duplicates`, IQR removal, `StandardScaler` — runs **before any split**, which is this project's #1 invariant; GDP↔Life-Expectancy at 0.82 is ignored while each regression plot is read separately; "0 correlation ⇒ the variables most likely do not affect each other" is what round 20 measured six counterexamples against. ## What happened before that (round 25)

Round 25 came from a single user-supplied URL — Analytics Vidhya's "Comprehensive & Practical Inferential Statistics Guide". **Every worked example's arithmetic verified correct** (CI, t=2.5, ANOVA F=25.1754, χ²=19.5833, regression F=188.3662), so the article's problems are conceptual; only two rounding slips (R² 0.758421 stated as 0.7588; χ² p 5.59e-5 stated as 0.0001). Roughly **90% of it was already covered here at higher rigor** — the value was in the three gaps, each measured before anything was written.

**(a) `associations.pairwise_group_differences` + `holm_bonferroni` (new).** The article recommends Fisher's LSD right after explaining alpha inflation. The methodological point of this measurement is that **the obvious experiment certifies LSD as safe**: under a *complete* null it holds 0.048–0.051 at every `g` (because the omnibus itself only fires 5% of the time, so the gate does all the work and the pairwise step is never reached), while uncorrected runs 0.1197 → **0.5068** at g=3→8. Under a *partial* null (one group really shifted, false positives counted only among truly-equal pairs) the omnibus fires **100%** of the time and LSD lands exactly on the uncorrected rate: 0.0488 / **0.1224** / **0.2036** / **0.2843** / **0.4374**. The partial null is the realistic EDA case. LSD survives only at g=3 (the classical result) and breaks from g=4. **BH is not a substitute** (0.0863 → 0.1752; it controls FDR, a different guarantee). Holm holds 0.038–0.049 in both regimes and dominates Bonferroni for free. Also: "three t-tests ≈ 15%" is the *independent* formula (0.1426); measured **0.1197**, since the pairs share groups.

**(b) The CLT threshold is about skewness, not `n` — and it corrected this project's own guidance.** `skew(mean) = skew(pop)/√n` verified exactly. At the advertised n=50 the 95% t-interval covers 0.934 (exponential), **0.900** (lognormal(0,1)), **0.695** (lognormal(0,2), still 0.874 at n=2000), while symmetric distributions are nominal at n=10. **The failure is one-sided**: at n=50 the interval sits entirely *below* the truth 9.4% of the time versus 0.28% above on lognormal(0,1) — a confident understatement, because a small right-skewed sample misses the large values that carry the mean. **The correction to existing text:** `distributions-outliers.md` called the percentile bootstrap "assumption-free, the default"; measured, it is *worse* than the t-interval on small samples — 0.890 vs **0.951** at n=10 on a **normal** population — and BCa recovers only ~1 pp. No interval formula can recover mass that was never drawn. Requalified, with the remedy stated as more rows / a different estimand / model the family.

**(c) `√R²` is not the correlation coefficient** — measured `r = −0.904853` against `√R² = +0.904853`; in multiple regression `√R² = 0.8375` matches no pair (`+0.5919/−0.5084/+0.2539`) and exceeds all of them.

**Not adopted, reported to the user instead** (already covered or not worth code): CLT stated with "variance = σ/√n" (that is the SE, a sd); SE defined as "variation in the sample data"; "changing n makes the sampling distribution no longer bell shaped" (backwards); margin of error defined as *surveyor* error (it covers no collection bias at all); Type II error as "acceptance of a false null", contradicting the article's own correct "we cannot accept the null", with an incoherent example and no mention of power; "greater n ⇒ greater chance of rejection" stated as a virtue. **Its motivating example is its own worst case**: class 40 → 45 "after extra classes" is a single-group pre/post with no control, where `regression_to_mean` and the round-20 time-validity threats apply and the article never says so. Credit where due: its confidence-interval interpretation bullet is correct and better than most introductions.

## What happened before that (rounds 13–24)

**Overlapping labels, and why the obvious correction is wrong.** A label defined over a forward span (return over the next 20 days, churn within 90 days, readmission within 30) makes rows `t` and `t+1` share `h−1` of `h` ingredients. Two quantities that get conflated: **uniqueness `1/h` is the right sample WEIGHT** (verified exactly: measured 0.202/0.052/0.022 vs 0.200/0.050/0.020 at h=5/20/50), but the right **variance inflation** is `deff = 1 + (h−1)(2h−1)/(3h)` → `2h/3`, **not `h`**. Measured false-positive rate for a correlation between two *independent* forward-window series (α=0.05, n=1000): naive 0.049 / 0.290 / 0.580 / **0.740** at h=1/5/20/50, while correcting by `h` collapses α to 0.011 and would silently delete real features; the formula holds 0.045–0.051. I shipped the **exact** Bartlett form rather than the `2h/3` limit I first measured, and that matters at small h — at h=2 exact gives α=0.057 vs 0.071, and at h=1 exact returns 1.0 where `2h/3` would claim 0.67 for data with no overlap at all.

**Purging is an honest null against its own reputation — do not "fix" this.** On a design where true skill is **exactly zero by construction**, apparent out-of-sample correlation was: shuffled 5-fold **+0.151 / +0.203 / +0.205**, contiguous 5-fold −0.026 / −0.048 / −0.116, purged+embargoed −0.027 / −0.047 / −0.127 (h=5/20/50, 25–40 reps, SE ≈0.01–0.02). **Shuffling is the catastrophic error**; contiguity does nearly all the repair; explicit purging added nothing measurable beyond contiguity, because shuffling's damage is interleaving training rows *among* test rows, not the fold boundary. Purging still ships as the principled guard but is documented as *not* a rescue for a shuffled split. Also: contiguous k-fold carries a **negative** bias at large h (−0.116), so a single CV number on overlapping labels is unreliable in **both** directions — report the spread (per-rep sd ≈0.13 at h=50). An earlier single-chronological-split test could not show purging's value at all (only h of ~360 test rows leak); re-running as k-fold was the fix, and the null survived it.

**Base-rate arithmetic (S3E16)**, verified against simulation at N=300k (formula vs simulated PPV 0.692/0.693, 0.321/0.318, 0.155/0.159 — the episode's 69% and 32% are exact). Now in `consistency-validity.md` + `imbalance-probes.md`: precision collapses at low prevalence (PPV 0.155 at prev 0.02 for a 90/90 detector, so ~85% of positive labels are wrong); **a noisy label inflates the apparent base rate 5.8×** (0.116 flagged at a true 0.02), which every imbalance decision taken from the label's own frequency inherits; and **specificity, not sensitivity, is the lever for a rare class** (0.80/0.999 → PPV 0.890 at prev 0.01). **Correction to the episode:** it dwells on the wide CI of the quoted sensitivity, but propagating it moves PPV only 0.318→0.336 while prevalence 0.20→0.02 moves it 0.692→0.155 — prevalence uncertainty dominates by an order of magnitude.

## Environment gotchas worth not re-discovering

- **`math.wm.edu` (Leemis distribution chart) has an incomplete cert chain.** Standard `WebFetch` fails with "unable to verify the first certificate". `curl --ssl-no-revoke` works (user has explicitly authorized this) and still validates the chain — the underlying issue is Windows schannel can't reach the revocation server, not an actual bad cert. Do not use `-k`/`--insecure`.
- **PDF extraction**: `pdftoppm`/poppler are absent, so the `Read` tool's page-rendering path for PDFs fails. Use `pdfplumber` via Bash/Python instead. Two-column academic papers need `page.crop((0, 0, w/2, h))` per column or the text interleaves into garbage.
- **Podcast transcription**: no ASR/torch/ffmpeg in this env by default. `pip install faster-whisper` (pulls ctranslate2 + onnxruntime, no ffmpeg needed) works. Model `small.en`, `device="cpu", compute_type="int8"` is cached at `C:\Users\felko\.cache\huggingface` (463 MB, `models--Systran--faster-whisper-small.en`) — **no re-download needed**. Measured throughput on this box: **0.46× real time** (170 min of audio in 78.6 min), so budget ~30 min per hour-long episode. Run via `run_in_background: true`. MP3 ID3 tags carry no description/chapters — nothing to mine without transcribing. Episode length is **not** inferable from file size without checking the bitrate: these episodes are 96 kbps, not the 128 kbps a size-based guess assumes, which understated their duration by a third. Read the MPEG frame header, or just trust the duration faster-whisper writes into the transcript header.
- **Transcription performance, measured — do not repeat the dead ends, and do not repeat the GPU over-estimate either.** (a) **Multiprocessing is a dead end here**: the CPU is an i5-7300HQ, **4 physical cores, no hyper-threading**, and one transcription process already averages 2.47 of them, with only ~2.1 GB RAM free. Three parallel processes would contend for ~1.5 spare cores and risk swapping — realistic ceiling ~1.3×, not 3×. Subagents would add nothing at all: they are Claude instances, not cores. (b) **GPU works but the real speedup is 1.91×, not the 5–8× estimated from spec sheets before measuring — write down the measured number, not the reasoned-from-first-principles one.** There is a GTX 1050 Ti (4 GB, driver 582.66, compute capability 6.1); `ctranslate2` 4.8.1 sees it and `get_supported_compute_types("cuda")` returns `{float32, int8_float32, int8}` — **`int8` works via Pascal `dp4a`; `float16` is absent from that list and would be slower than CPU** (Pascal fp16 throughput is 1/64 of fp32), so the reflex "GPU ⇒ float16" backfires here. **Getting CUDA to actually run took two fixes, in this order:** first `RuntimeError: Library cublas64_12.dll is not found` (not a cuDNN error as originally guessed) — fixed by `pip install nvidia-cublas-cu12 nvidia-cudnn-cu12` (~700 MB, confirmed installed and working); second, those pip-installed DLLs sit under `site-packages\nvidia\*\bin`, which is **not** on the default search path — a Python-side fix is required before `WhisperModel(device="cuda")` will find them:
  ```python
  import os, site
  from pathlib import Path
  for base in site.getsitepackages() + [site.getusersitepackages()]:
      for bindir in (Path(base) / "nvidia").glob("*/bin"):
          os.add_dll_directory(str(bindir))
  ```
  On `small.en` (250 MB int8), this gave CPU 0.437× vs GPU 0.229× real time on the identical 120s clip — **1.91×**, confirmed by direct A/B on the same audio, not an estimate. (c) **`large-v3` (2.9 GB download) does not fit in 4 GB VRAM at `beam_size=5`** — crashed with `CUDA failed with error out of memory` after ~35 min of runaway compute (the default temperature-fallback retry loop multiplies cost when quality thresholds aren't met, which is almost certainly why it ran so much longer than the audio length before failing). Fits fine at **`beam_size=1, temperature=0.0`** (no fallback loop): 150s audio in 65.6s (0.438×, in line with the small.en ratio). **This is the actionable warning: reduced beam_size to fit VRAM measurably costs quality.** Word-level diff of that clip against the existing small.en (beam=5) CPU transcript of the identical timestamps (`difflib`, similarity 0.864): large-v3 fixed one real small.en error ("nice place" → correct "niche place") but **introduced a 16-word hallucinated clause** ("...these situations, I think it's really important to be able to understand the statistics and the statistics...") that has no counterpart in the source audio or the CPU transcript — a known large-model failure mode, plausibly worsened by disabling beam search and the fallback loop that would normally catch a low-confidence decode. **Net verdict: on this sample, large-v3-at-beam=1 is a wash against small.en-at-beam=5, not a quality win** — re-transcribing the existing episodes is **not justified** by what was measured. If GPU-quality large-v3 is wanted later, the fix is architectural (chunk audio into shorter windows to keep beam=5's memory footprint under 4 GB, not simply "use the GPU"), and that was not attempted. (d) `faster_whisper.BatchedInferencePipeline` exists in 1.2.1 and was **not** used in any of this; it typically gives 2–4× within a single process on CPU and was not tried on GPU either — still open.

## Working pattern this session used (repeat it)

For every external source (course PDFs, a chart+FAQ, podcast episodes): extract/verify the claim on synthetic data with a known ground truth *before* writing it into a reference or script — several claims from otherwise-reliable sources turned out to be over-broad, mis-parameterized, or (in one case) a genuine bug in this project's own code that the source's framing happened to expose. Every reference file that resulted has the measured numbers inline, not just the citation. Keep that standard. After any script or reference change: re-run the full smoke test, then rebuild whichever `dist/*.zip` changed, then check `gpt_instructions.md`'s byte budget if that file was touched.
